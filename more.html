tell their stories, we force shame out of hiding, and end the silence" - Brené Brown

Tu veux en parler ? ("Do you want to talk about it?") is a cards game that seeks to facilitate dialogue around mental health, a topic that affects us all, yet so tricky to talk about. With 120 questions split in 3 categories, this tool guides players towards kind, nuanced and healing conversations.




Early game with one of the first iteration - January 2021



While I had been interested in learning about mental health for a while, it was only three years ago that I realised the importance of being vulnerable about it. I had just returned to Belgium after 2 years living in Toronto. There, I had found myself surrounded by people who talked casually about their mental health: my design lead sharing about anxiety and stress, my company offering mental health days, nationwide awareness campains, friends discussing self-care during depressive episodes,... It left an impression on me and I started being more open myself, even once back in Brussels. I started noticing something: there would always be a couple of people who would come to me afterwards, privately, to thank me and share their experience. As in people wanted to talk about mental health but just needed a trigger. It made me wonder how to systematize this process. How to normalize awkward and vulnerable conversations? what I’m fascinated with is difficult conversations. The way we muddling though them, the moments of vulnerability that unlock them, the way someone says something and you know you’re not alone.

Cue to a year later, everything is locking downm we’re isolating and working from home. "Connecting" becomes a buzzword and it seems every week someone else is going on sick leave for burn out. I asked question. On my socials first, then expanded. People were candid and open in those anonymous and same direction: we want to talk about it. But how? became a proper user research with hundreds of people who answered online questionnaire, then over 20 1hour talk with people. Mental health is at the heart of the discussions of the 18-25 generation. The one that is already being called the "covid generation". We have gone from a completely taboo, denied, minimized topic to a generational discussion. If the current climate shows all the issues related to the subject, this discussion started long before the health crisis. Just look at the number of responses we received to our call for testimony: young people are interested in their mental health and are not afraid to talk about it. We can also look to social media: supportive Instagram accounts are flourishing. Young people are vlogging their darker moments on YouTube. Others have made the topic a trend on TikTok.
