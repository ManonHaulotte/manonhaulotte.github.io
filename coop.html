A dense, overwhelming Information Architecture

Buying car insurance is overwhelming for anyone â€” but especially for first-time drivers. One of the core challenges we faced was the sheer amount of information users needed to process. Insurance is inherently dense: legal disclosures, detailed policy options, and technical terms abound. Yet first-time drivers - our core audience â€” often lacked even basic reference points. We needed to educate them, help them make good decisions, and guide them smoothly through the quote and purchase process, all without overwhelming them.

So we collaborated with our users and the relevant internal departments to redefine the information architecture. Our research phase involved a lot of card sorting and information architecture workshops, both internally and with users. Through open and closed card sorts, we discovered that users naturally grouped information by task, not by the internal categories the company had historically used. Terms like "deductible" or "endorsement" confused users; they wanted clear, relatable language and progressive layers of detail only when they felt ready for it.

We mapped out an entirely new information architecture focused on a progressive disclosure model, revealing information only when needed to reduce cognitive load.

Exploration, Testing & Iterations
Design Solution:
Progressive Disclosure & Information Layering

These insights led us to reimagine the information architecture around a progressive disclosure model. We prioritized what users needed immediately, simplified language whenever possible, and designed the experience to let users dive deeper into specifics at their own pace.

Throughout the design process, we ran usability tests at different stages of fidelity â€” from rough wireframes to near-final prototypes. We saw early on that users disengaged when faced with heavy blocks of legal text, but when we implemented expandable "Learn More" modules, users felt more in control and better informed. Simple progress indicators and encouraging microcopy also played an outsized role in keeping users moving forward without second-guessing themselves.

One of the most difficult and rewarding parts of the redesign was the final quote presentation. Insurance quotes are notoriously confusing, especially for newcomers. After multiple rounds of testing and iteration, we landed on a three-options view: a Basic Coverage option for those focused on affordability, a Recommended Coverage that balanced protection and value, and an Enhanced Coverage for users seeking maximum security. Each option included a clean monthly price display, key coverage highlights, and expandable sections for detailed breakdowns. We also introduced a side-by-side comparison feature, empowering users to confidently weigh their choices.

Working closely with developers and experts in an Agile environment meant that UX was integrated into every sprint. I collaborated directly with developers on writing user stories and acceptance criteria, sat in sprint planning meetings to help scope features realistically, and frequently synced with legal teams to make sure compliance wasn't just an afterthought.

In parallel, I worked closely with The Co-operators' branding and marketing teams to ensure that the redesigned onboarding experience aligned with their updated brand guidelines, as well as their in-house UX writer. We paid special attention to the tone of voice, imagery, and micro-interactions, making sure they all contributed to a welcoming and reassuring atmosphere - critical for young drivers making a major financial decision for the first time.

Although the full onboarding flow was still in final QA testing when I transitioned off the project, early beta results were extremely promising. Users were completing quotes 23% faster, quote-to-purchase conversions for first-time drivers had risen by 15%, and there was a 40% reduction in customer support tickets related to onboarding confusion. Perhaps most importantly, internal stakeholders, especially in legal and compliance, expressed a new confidence that users genuinely understood what they were buying, something that had been a long-standing concern.
