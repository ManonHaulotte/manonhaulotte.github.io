How might we facilitate greater public engagement with academic research?

Created during the immersive UX Design program at Brainstation Toronto, biblio aims to increase the reach and impact of academic research by making the findings easy to discover, comprehend and engage with outside of universities. This project gave me the opportunity to explore each step of the design process, from identifying and understanding the barriers to engagement, to finding and testing possible solutions to overcome them.

DISCOVERY
& RESEARCH
The problem:
Academic findings are confined to an increasingly narrow audience.

Idea for an opportunity We want to create a clear, functional and personalized dashboard to facilitate users’ workflow by: highlighting urgent actions that need taking care of, allowing them to quickly complete frequently accessed tasks, enabling them to monitor key information at a glance and offering curated and relevant content. (Idea) While existing research does hint at a demand for a dashboard, it mostly confirms two problems we’re trying to solve: users have difficulties finding what they need to do on BelfiusWeb, and they see the current landing page as an unnecessary step (this is reflected in the web analytics and in the clients complains as well).

The current state of the landing page fails to provide any added value and is perceived as an unnecessary click. We believe a dashboard will fill this gap by aligning with users’ needs and workflows. Our initial focus will be on facilitating the daily tasks for the executing staff, before focusing on creating value for the decisions makers. (Opportunity Statement)

We believe this will increase users’ satisfaction and loyalty by, on one hand, making the executing staff more successful in their role and, on the other hand, providing value and creating trust for the decision makers. We know this is true when we see an increase in both the frequency and depth of use of BelfiusWeb. (Hypothesis)

Exploration, Testing & Iterations
The solution:
Shorter, simpler, personalized.

We conducted 4 workshops to:  Define the business value of our idea, with an impact map.  Better understand the users’ needs and workflow by creating provisional personas.  Generate ideas and determine  the ideal vision state.  Prioritize our ideas and agree on intermediary steps, with an impact vs effort matrix.

We chose to prioritize three profiles of users: Decision Makers: Responsables Entités (Overall Growth)  Decision Makers: Responsables Financiers (Financial Health)  Executing Staff (Daily banking)  Then, with the help of corporate bankers and e-consultants, we created provisional personas for each of those categories (= our best guesses as to who our users are, how they’re using BW and what their goals and pain points are, without having done proper user research)

  • At the end of the exercise, we realized that we had 4 profiles with very specific and very different tasks and needs, but all using the same tool and all having the same request: to only see what’s relevant for them, with as little unnecessary steps and content as possible.  Based on this knowledge, we moved on to an ideation exercice, to answer those 4 questions:  How might we facilitate the users’ recurring tasks? How might we meet the users’ personalization needs? How might we prompt the users to connect every day to BelfiusWeb? How might we continuously provide content that’s relevant to each user?
  • We defined quick wins and big projects, and identified three steps: First, we’ll create a dashboard that the users can personalize themselves and that includes: shortcuts to top tasks, recent activity (alerts, notifications, documents to sign,…), an overview on relevant accounts, etc. It’ll focus on facilitating the daily tasks of the executing staff.  Then, we'll shift the focus to providing added value to the decision makers, offering them an actionable overview of the company's financial health and growth with relevant KPIs  The long term vision is to provide a feed of relevant content and to anticipate users’ needs As we go along, we’ll learn more about the users and offer more customization..

We need to validate our assumptions and research the unknowns in three areas: UX: We will need to validate the users’ key tasks and workflow, as well as better define the various profiles, to ensure the dashboard actually creates value.  Data and IT: How to identify users’ profiles? How to capture the user's preferences? How to stock and use them to show relevant content? etc. We need to answer those quickly, to create a future-proof solution from the beginning. Communication: Where will the content come from?

Visual identity & marketing website
biblio:
The final product.

When it was time to pick a name, colours, typefaces and an illustration style, I made sure it reflected what the app was about: simplicity, discovery, community. As academia is often perceived to be obscure and intimadating, I wanted a brand that appeared friendly and modern, but also trustworthy.

This is what I aimed to convey on the marketing website as well, with a minimalist layout, one-line illustrations and clear explanations. While we were only asked to design the page, I wanted to bring biblio to life and decided to code it. Although the result is far from perfect, as I only had two days to work on it, I enjoyed the challenge and people liked interacting with the website when we showcased our projects.

LEARNINGS & NEXT STEPS
What's next:
Involving the researchers.

I felt proud at the end of this project: I had managed to take a complex issue through a 2 months design process and was getting a lot of positive feedback for the final product. I had also learned a lot: how to listen to users instead of blindly following my first ideas, how to focus on perfecting the core flow rather than wanting to "design it all", how to trust the process and not get so attached to my initial designs, how to keep going when it gets overwhelming, etc.

However, there's still so much to be improved. With the flow, design and copy of both the app and the website of course, but more importantly, I need to start including researchers. Indeed, academia still operate under an ancient system that values only scholarly output and has yet to find an incentive for researchers to engage the public. Even when they want to, they might not know how or simply not have the time. By working with academics, I could better grasp the complexities of their process and uncover what would encourage them to share their findings with the wider audience in an accessible format.
