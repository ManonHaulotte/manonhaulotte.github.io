Corporate banking app: a redesign gone wrong.

Belfius is the leading Belgian bank, and their private banking app ranks high with customers, thanks to a great design team.

However, one department was having issues: the PCB team, who develops and maintains the digital solutions for corporate and public banking. They had been collaborating with an external agency to redesign their web app for the past 2 years. It should be noted that we're talking about a platform that people in various financial roles (accountants, CFO's, investments managers,â€¦) and in companies ranging from small local businesses to multimillion international corporations need to use to complete their daily tasks. An unclear navigation or poor user experience therefore means time lost during the work day, constant frustration for the employees and real financial consequences for our clients.

Unfortunately, the redesign had prioritized the look and feel, with little to no consideration for user needs and context. The big launch was not going as planned: users were confused by the new version of BelfiusWeb, the support team was receiving too many calls everyday and corporate clients weren't happy.

That's when I was brought on board. It was the first time a UX designer would work with this team and on this product.



For a month, I mostly observed. I worked on small design tasks, while getting to know the many stakeholders, asking many questions to the user researchers and the customers support team, learning from the domain experts and familiarizing myself with the complex and multilayered platform. It became obvious that the product reflected the team organization: different stakeholders with varying visions were working in silos, domain and user knowledge was scattered accross departments, and a proper design process needed to be formalized.

So I got the team together, with some key external stakeholders, and we defined five design principles for BelfiusWeb. Not only was this a fun workshop to facilitate, it created a shared design vision that everyone felt they could own, introduced a different way of working and proved tremendously helpful with decision making later on.

USABILITY ANALYSIS

Armed with the team's shared design vision, as well as a set of heuristic and insights from previous user research, I began a thorough usability evaluation of BelfiusWeb.

This shared document informed future design effort and pointed to a critical issue: the way information was organised (information architecture), connected (primary navigation) and contextualized (local navigation) on BelfiusWeb was causing a lot of friction.

A (sad) fun fact to illustrate this: when a colleague visited one of our clients' office, he reported that one employee had a sticky note next to her laptop, where she'd written the step-by-step path to follow to complete her key tasks on the app.

USER RESEARCH

This audit highlighted another underlying problem: a lack of first-hand knowledge and contextual understanding of what users need, how they work, what their frustrations are, etc.

I mentionned earlier that we were designing a professional tool that had to enable a large variety of user profiles to be efficient and successful in their role, and that frustrating interactions had a tangible negative impact on their working life. As designers, this meant a couple of inherent complexities:

  • A complex and layered information architecture: how do we make the workflows of our all diverse users (from an accountant in a small non-profit to the CFO of a large corp) relevant and intuitive?
  • Varying contexts and usages: the web app could be used on a bad quality screen, for several hours througough the day, in a badly lit office, or on the go, on a smartphone, to confirm one transaction, for example.
  • Limited access to users: because some users were "VIP" corporate clients and couldn't be interviewed or surveyed, there were gaps in our user knowledge.

In an effort to identify those blind spots, I created the template for the following matrix and filled it up with all the user insights I had gathered, from the general (e.g. mid corp users, accountants) to the specific (e.g. an accountant in a midcorp), differenciating analytics and research from assumptions.

I then passed it on to user researchers Glenn Stevens and Maxime Deroeck, who conducted user interviews in order to fill the gaps and verify assumptions.
